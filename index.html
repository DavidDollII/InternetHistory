1969: The First Connection

The internet began as ARPANET, a military project. On October 29, 1969, a message was sent from a computer at UCLA to another at Stanford Research Institute. The intended message was "LOGIN," but the system crashed after "LO." This humble beginning marked the first host-to-host connection of what would become the internet. Building the Foundation (1970s - 1980s)

The decades after the first connection were about building the fundamental protocols that would allow diverse networks to communicate. This period saw the invention of the internet's common language and the groundwork for its civilian use.

73

TCP/IP Invented

Vint Cerf and Robert Kahn design TCP/IP, the core communication protocols that create a true 'network of networks' by standardizing how data is sent and received.

83

Domain Name System (DNS)

The familiar .com, .org, and .edu system is introduced, replacing complex numerical addresses with human-readable names, making the network far more accessible.

85

NSFNET is Born

The National Science Foundation creates a high-speed 'backbone' network, connecting universities and research centers, effectively becoming the internet's new infrastructure and opening it to wider academic use.

1991: The Web is Woven

Working at CERN, British scientist Tim Berners-Lee invents the World Wide Web. He develops HTML, HTTP, and the first web browser, combining them into a system for easily sharing and accessing information. This wasn't a new internet, but a revolutionary way to use it, turning it from a data-transfer tool into a universe of interconnected pages.

The Explosive Growth of Websites

After the web's invention, the number of websites grew from a single site in 1991 to millions by the end of the decade, signaling the start of the digital gold rush.

The Shift to Mobile

The launch of the iPhone in 2007 heralded the mobile era. Today, mobile devices account for the majority of website traffic, fundamentally changing how we access and interact with the internet.
